Approach

Social Thinking®

Funshine Speech Therapy uses vocabulary, materials, and methods from the Social Thinking® curriculum created by Michelle Garcia Winner, a speech-language pathologist who specializes in social cognitive development. The framework helps individuals with social learning challenges become better social thinkers.

What is Social Thinking?

Social thinking is what we do whenever we share space with others — in a classroom, at the grocery store, during a conversation, or even while reading fiction. It involves reading context, understanding others' intentions and emotions, and adjusting our own behavior in response. For most people this process develops naturally; for many individuals with social learning challenges, it needs to be explicitly taught.

Key concepts at a glance

  • Social thinking begins developing from birth — joint attention is one of its earliest milestones.
  • Effective peer play in early childhood builds the foundation for classroom learning and conversation.
  • Children with social learning challenges need explicit, cognitive instruction — not just modeled behavior.
  • Social Thinking strategies are designed for individuals with average to above-average verbal IQ.
  • Core concepts include perspective-taking, reading the room, and understanding how our behavior affects others' thoughts and feelings.
  • Social thinking is relevant throughout life — in friendships, workplaces, and community settings.
  • Individuals who learn how to think socially can generalize that skill to new environments — unlike those who only learn specific social behaviors.
  • Social learning challenges frequently co-occur with anxiety, depression, and OCD; treatment often addresses these connections.

The Hanen Program®

Marisa Whitman is Hanen Certified, which means she is trained to lead the "It Takes Two to Talk®" parent program — one of the most well-researched approaches to early language development.

About The Hanen Centre

Founded more than 35 years ago, The Hanen Centre is a Canadian charitable organization dedicated to helping parents, caregivers, early childhood educators, and speech-language pathologists support young children's language, social, and literacy development — including children with or at risk of language delays and those with Autism Spectrum Disorder.

Practical Tips

Supporting your child at home

Small, consistent actions in everyday life make a meaningful difference in language development. Here are some evidence-based strategies our therapists recommend:

  • Follow your child's lead — Let their interest guide the activity. Learning happens best when children are engaged and motivated.
  • Wait and listen — Give your child extra time to respond before jumping in. Resist the urge to fill silence.
  • Expand what they say — If your child says "ball," you might respond "big red ball!" to model slightly more complex language.
  • Comment, don't quiz — Rather than constantly asking "what's that?", narrate what you and your child are doing together.
  • Read together daily — Even brief shared reading sessions dramatically support vocabulary and listening comprehension.
  • Limit screen time — Back-and-forth human interaction is irreplaceable for early language development.
